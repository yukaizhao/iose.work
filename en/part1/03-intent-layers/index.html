      Chapter 3: Intent Layers

      1 Why Layering is Necessary

      Intent is never a simple, single-dimensional instruction, but rather a layered semantic system. Let us understand this through a concrete scenario: when an executive says “we want to develop a membership system” at a project kickoff meeting, different roles in the room actually receive completely different information—

      The CEO envisions numbers on financial reports: can this system improve user retention? Can it generate substantial revenue growth? How does it support the company’s long-term competitive moat?

      The Product Manager is thinking about the functional blueprint: how many membership tiers should there be? How should benefits be designed for each tier? What pricing gradient should be adopted? How should it integrate with the existing points system?

      The Technical Lead’s focus has already shifted to the technical level: how should this system ensure performance? How is data security guaranteed? How does it remain stable under high-concurrency scenarios?

      The same concept of a “membership system” presents entirely different aspects in the cognition of different roles. This cognitive difference is not a communication failure, but rather a manifestation of the inherent complexity of software systems—it simultaneously involves multiple dimensions such as business value, product functionality, and technical constraints.

      The core purpose of intent layering is precisely to establish order within this complexity. It decomposes the vague “develop a system” into three clear layers: Business Intent specifies the direction of value, Product Intent defines specific functionality, and Technical Intent delineates implementation boundaries. Among these, Product Intent and Technical Intent are the core operational interfaces of the IOSE transformation.

      Business Intent

      Business Intent focuses on macro-level business objectives, answering the question of “why we should do this.” It typically comes from senior management and points the direction for the entire organization.

      Core Elements of Business Intent:

      ElementDescriptionExample
      Business ObjectiveDesired business outcome to achieveIncrease annual membership renewal rate by 10%
      Value PropositionCore value created for the organizationReduce passive churn, enhance user stickiness
      Strategic ContextWhy this needs to be done nowIntensifying market competition, membership retention becoming a key metric

      Business Intent is relatively stable and serves more as background context. In the IOSE transformation, it does not need to be frequently manipulated, but needs to be clearly understood and communicated.

      Business Intent Example:

      What we want to do:
Increase the renewal rate of annual members

Business Objectives:
- Increase the annual membership renewal rate from the current 75% to 85%,
  i.e., reduce approximately 2,000 passive churn users per month
- Through proactive service methods, enable more members nearing expiration
  to complete renewal in a timely manner
- Expected annual revenue growth of approximately 5 million yuan

Value Proposition:
Value to the company:
Reducing user churn means higher customer lifetime value. Each retained annual
member can bring an average of 3 additional years of subscription revenue.
Compared to the high cost of acquiring new customers, retaining existing users
has a higher ROI.

Value to users:
Help users avoid benefit interruptions caused by forgetting or oversight,
ensuring they can continuously enjoy membership privileges, and enhancing
user loyalty and satisfaction with the platform.

Strategic Context:
- Intensifying market competition: Over the past two years, customer acquisition
  costs in the same industry have risen by 40%. The growth model relying solely
  on new user acquisition is no longer sustainable

- Company strategic transformation: This year, the company explicitly proposed
  a transformation from "traffic-oriented" to "user value-oriented," with
  existing user operations becoming the core strategy

- Data-discovered problems: User research shows that over 60% of churned members
  indicated "forgetting to renew" as the main reason, rather than dissatisfaction
  with the service. This means there is huge room for recovery

      Product Intent

      Product Intent is the bridge connecting business and technology. It transforms abstract business objectives into concrete product functional solutions, answering the question of “what to do.”

      Core Elements of Product Intent:

      • Functional Goals: What specific functionality to implement and what problems to solve
      • Target Users: Who is the audience for this feature, and what are their characteristics
      • Usage Scenarios: Under what circumstances is the feature used, and what are the trigger conditions
      • User Experience: How do users perceive this feature, and what is the operation path
      • Acceptance Criteria: How to measure whether the feature is successful, and what quantifiable metrics exist

      Product Intent is one of the core operational objects of the IOSE transformation. In the IOSE paradigm, the product manager’s focus shifts from writing PRDs and drawing prototypes to defining structured Product Intent. This structured Product Intent can directly drive AI to generate runnable software, giving product personnel the ability to directly create digital products.

      Product Intent has two main forms of expression: text descriptions are used to express functional logic and business processes, while Figma designs are used to precisely express user interfaces.

      Text Expression: Describing Functionality and Processes

      Text format is suitable for describing functional logic, business processes, algorithm rules, etc., that do not involve complex interfaces. It uses natural language to clearly articulate “what to do,” “who it’s for,” “in what scenarios it’s used,” and “how success is measured.”

      Text-based Product Intent Example:

      Functional Goals:
Develop an expiration reminder feature for annual membership benefits. When
members' benefits are about to expire, reach out through multiple channels
including App push notifications and email to remind users to renew in a
timely manner, thereby reducing passive churn caused by forgetting.

Target Users:
Annual members, especially those within 30 days of expiration. According to
data analysis, this group of users has high renewal willingness but is prone
to benefit interruptions due to forgetting.

Usage Scenarios:
When a member's annual benefits are about to expire within 30 days, the system
needs to proactively initiate reminders:
- 30 days before expiration: First friendly reminder, informing that benefits
  are about to expire
- 7 days before expiration: Second reminder, emphasizing benefit value and
  renewal discounts
- 1 day before expiration: Final reminder, creating urgency
Reminder channels include App push notifications and email.

User Experience:
- Clear reminder content: Clearly inform about expiration time, remaining
  benefit value, and what will be lost without renewal
- Minimal operation path: Provide a one-click renewal shortcut, allowing users
  to complete payment without multiple redirects
- Respect user choice: Provide options for "remind again in 7 days" and
  "do not remind again" to avoid excessive disturbance

Acceptance Criteria:
Functional Accuracy:
- Reminder trigger timing is accurate, triggered at 30 days, 7 days, and 1 day
  before expiration respectively
- Reminder recipient filtering is correct, excluding users who have already
  renewed or disabled auto-renewal

Business Effectiveness:
- Reminder open rate > 60% (i.e., over 60% of users who receive reminders will
  click to view)
- Renewal conversion rate > 15% (i.e., over 15% of users who click to view will
  complete renewal)
- User complaint rate < 1% (complaints caused by excessive disturbance or
  inaccurate reminders)

      Figma Expression: Precisely Describing User Interfaces

      When Product Intent involves user interfaces, pure text descriptions often struggle to precisely convey visual details—the specific position of buttons, color values, font sizes, element spacing, and other information described in words are both lengthy and prone to ambiguity. In this case, Figma designs are the best tool for expressing interface intent.

      The essential advantage of Figma lies in its definition of design as data:

      • Traditional design tools output pixels (images). Large models need to “understand” visual elements and reason about their structure and style. This process not only consumes significant computing power but also easily loses details (such as precise spacing, color values, font parameters).
      • Every interface element in Figma—from button position coordinates, color values to font and spacing parameters—is structured data. Figma’s file format is essentially a form of “pseudocode” that can be precisely read and programmatically manipulated.

      Therefore, when Product Intent includes interfaces, prioritize providing Figma design links or exported structured data rather than screenshots or pure text descriptions. This allows AI to accurately reproduce design intent rather than guessing from vague visual information.

      Interface-based Product Intent Example:

      Functional Goals:
Design push notification cards and email pages for membership renewal reminders
to increase user click-through rates and renewal conversion rates.

Interface Deliverables:
- Figma design link: https://figma.com/file/xxx/member-renewal
- Or exported structured data file (such as JSON generated by figma-to-code plugin)

Key Interface Descriptions:
1. App Push Notification Card
   - Display location: Phone lock screen and notification center
   - Content elements: Membership tier icon, benefit expiration countdown,
     renewal button
   - Interaction: Swipe left to close, click to enter renewal page

2. Email Reminder Page
   - Top: Brand logo and membership exclusive badge
   - Body: Benefit list (used/unused), expiration time, renewal discount
   - Bottom: One-click renewal button, customer service contact, unsubscribe link

Design Specifications:
- Follow iOS/Android official push notification design guidelines
- Email width limited to 600px to ensure compatibility across clients
- Button color uses brand primary color #FF6B35 to ensure clear visual hierarchy

Acceptance Criteria:
- Design covers both push cards and email formats completely
- All visual elements are annotated with clear dimensions, colors, and font parameters
- Provide adaptation solutions for mobile and desktop

      Technical Intent

      The core mission of Technical Intent is to transform Product Intent into concrete plans that coding Agents can understand and execute, while ensuring the implementation process meets quality boundaries. It answers two key questions: “how to make AI understand the requirements” and “how to do it right.”

      Technical Intent consists of two major components:

      Part One: Implementation Specification (How to Implement)

      This part focuses on breaking down product functionality into technical solutions that Agents can understand, and is the most transformative aspect of Technical Intent.

      ElementDescriptionExample
      Requirement DecompositionBreak down product features into independently implementable technical modulesBreak down into “Reminder Generator,” “Push Service,” and “Email Service” modules
      Technical DesignKey design decisions: architecture choices, data models, interface contractsAdopt event-driven architecture; define Reminder and Notification domain models
      Task BreakdownBreak down implementation work into development tasks that Agents can understandTask 1: Define data models; Task 2: Implement reminder generation logic
      Acceptance SpecificationCompletion criteria and validation methods for each taskUnit tests pass; interface contracts comply with OpenAPI specification

      Part Two: Quality Constraints (Constraints)

      This part defines boundary conditions that the implementation process must adhere to.

      ElementDescriptionExample
      Performance ConstraintsResponse time, throughput, resource consumptionInterface P99 response time < 50ms
      Security ConstraintsSecurity baselines and compliance requirementsAll interfaces must pass OAuth2.0 authentication
      Code Quality ConstraintsCode quality and maintainability standardsUnit test coverage > 80%
      Architecture ConstraintsSystem structural healthModule size, cyclomatic complexity, layering direction constraints
      Dependency ConstraintsTechnology stack and infrastructure limitationsMust use MySQL 8.0

      Special Note on Architecture Constraints:

      Architecture Constraints are a key mechanism in the IOSE paradigm for ensuring long-term software maintainability. When AI Agents continuously generate and modify code, without clear architecture boundary constraints, systems can easily fall into “chaotic growth”—blurred module responsibilities, complex and tangled dependency relationships, and uncontrolled code complexity. This not only affects human understanding but also significantly reduces the efficiency of AI Agents in subsequent work.

      Core Architecture Constraint Elements:

      • Layering Direction Constraints: Strictly define the dependency direction of layers in a layered architecture (such as the classic three-layer architecture: Controller → Service → Repository), prohibiting reverse dependencies or cross-layer calls. This ensures that AI does not break the clear boundaries of the architecture when generating new code.

      • Cyclomatic Complexity Control: Require that the cyclomatic complexity of each function/method does not exceed 10 (or the threshold agreed upon by the team). High-complexity code is not only difficult to maintain but also makes it difficult for AI to grasp logical boundaries during subsequent modifications, easily leading to bugs.

      • Module Size Constraints: Limit the number of lines of code in a single file, class, or function (such as no more than 500 lines per file, no more than 50 lines per function). Appropriate granularity allows AI to more accurately locate modification scopes, reduce the impact of changes, and improve generation efficiency.

      Through these constraints, we ensure that AI Agents always face a codebase with clear structure and well-defined boundaries during continuous work, thereby maintaining efficient code generation and modification capabilities.

      Technical Intent is the core operational object of the IOSE transformation. Developers shift from “personally writing every line of code” to “defining implementation specifications and reviewing AI execution.” High-quality Technical Intent should be clear enough for Agent tools like Cursor and Claude Code to autonomously complete development tasks based on it.

      Practical References for Technical Intent

      In actual work, the writing of Technical Intent can reference the following tools and concepts:

      Reference Cursor’s Agent Mode: Cursor’s Composer feature demonstrates how to transform requirement descriptions into multi-file code modifications. In Technical Intent, we need to provide a similar “context map”—explaining the responsibilities of each module, how they interact with other modules, and the organization structure of key files.

      Reference Claude Code’s Spec-Driven Approach: When handling complex tasks, Claude Code first confirms an “Implementation Plan” with the user. The “Task Breakdown” section in Technical Intent is similar to such an implementation plan, requiring clear inputs, outputs, and acceptance criteria for each subtask.

      Reference Kiro’s Spec-Driven Development: Kiro emphasizes “Specs as Code,” meaning specification documents themselves are executable constraints. The “Acceptance Specification” in Technical Intent should be precise enough to be verified by automated tools—such as through unit test assertions, API contract tests, or static code analysis to ensure implementation meets specifications.

      3 Interaction Relationships Between the Three Layers

      Business Intent, Product Intent, and Technical Intent are not three isolated islands, but an organic whole. Understanding the interaction relationships between them is crucial for effectively applying intent layering.

      Top-Down Transformation Process

      In actual software planning, high-level Business Intent needs to be progressively transformed into technical solutions that Agents can understand:

      flowchart TD
    A["Business Intent: Increase membership renewal rate by 10%"]
    B["Product Intent: Benefit expiration reminder feature"]
    C["Technical Intent A: Reminder generation service"]
    D["Technical Intent B: Push notification service"]
    E["Technical Intent C: Renewal page optimization"]

    A --> B
    B --> C
    B --> D
    B --> E

    style B fill:#e8f5e9,stroke:#388e3c,stroke-width:2px
    style C fill:#fff3e0,stroke:#f57c00,stroke-width:2px
    style D fill:#fff3e0,stroke:#f57c00,stroke-width:2px
    style E fill:#fff3e0,stroke:#f57c00,stroke-width:2px

      Business Intent answers “why we should do this,” providing direction guidance for Product Intent; Product Intent answers “what to do,” transforming business objectives into concrete functional solutions; Technical Intent answers “how to do it right,” setting quality boundaries for product implementation.

      Bottom-Up Support Relationship

      Conversely, each layer of intent provides implementation support for the layer above:

      • Technical Intent supports Product Intent: Without performance constraints, reminder services might crash during peak periods, and product functionality cannot operate normally; without security constraints, user data leaks would render product value meaningless.
      • Product Intent supports Business Intent: If product functionality is poorly designed and users are unwilling to use it, business objectives cannot be achieved; if acceptance criteria are improperly set and actual effects cannot be measured, business value cannot be verified.

      Product Intent and Technical Intent are the core operational interfaces of the IOSE transformation. Product personnel directly drive AI understanding of “what to do” by defining clear Product Intent; technical personnel ensure AI knows “how to do it right” by defining complete Technical Intent. Together, they constitute the main channel for human-AI collaboration.

      4 Precision Principles of Intent Expression

      The quality of intent expression directly determines whether AI Agents can accurately understand and execute. Here are two key principles:

      Avoiding Ambiguity: From Vague to Precise

      Natural language itself has inherent ambiguity, but intent documents require eliminating this ambiguity as much as possible. Here are some common comparison examples:

      Vague Expression (Avoid)Precise Expression (Recommended)
      “Complete as soon as possible”“Complete development within 3 working days, deploy within 1 week”
      “Good performance”“Interface P99 response time < 100ms, support 1000 QPS concurrency”
      “User-friendly experience”“Page first load time < 2 seconds, error messages in English with specific action recommendations”
      “Ensure security”“All APIs must pass OAuth2.0 authentication, sensitive data encrypted with AES-256”
      “System should be stable”“System availability > 99.9%, fault recovery time < 30 minutes”

      Verifiability: Ensuring Intent Can Be Verified

      Every intent should be objectively verifiable. If an intent cannot be verified, it cannot serve as an execution target and acceptance criterion for AI Agents.

      Poor Intent Example: “The system should run stably”

      Problem: “Stable” is a subjective concept; different people may have different interpretations

      Improved Intent: “System availability > 99.9%, fault recovery time < 30 minutes, unplanned downtime < 43 minutes per month”

      Advantage: Clear metrics, measurable, verifiable

      5 Chapter Summary

      Intent layering is the theoretical cornerstone and practical guide of Intent-Oriented Software Engineering. Through this chapter, we should master the following core points:

      1. Business Intent answers “why do it”—it points the direction of value and provides background for Product Intent;
      2. Product Intent answers “what to do”—it transforms business objectives into concrete functional solutions and is the core operational object of the IOSE transformation;
      3. Technical Intent answers “how to make AI understand and implement”—it includes Implementation Specifications (Requirement Decomposition, Technical Design, Task Breakdown) and Quality Constraints, and is the technical core of the IOSE transformation;
      4. The precision expression principle eliminates the ambiguity of natural language, ensuring that intent can be accurately understood and verified by AI.

      The benefits of this three-layer structure are multifaceted:

      • Business personnel can focus on value direction without getting bogged down in functional details;
      • Product personnel can focus on functional definition, directly driving development through structured intent;
      • Technical personnel shift from code executors to specification definers, guiding AI to complete implementation through Technical Intent;
      • AI Agents receive clear, structured, understandable input, capable of autonomously completing the entire process from design to coding.

      Core Insight: In the IOSE paradigm, the value of technical personnel is no longer reflected in “speed of writing code” but in “ability to define clear specifications.” An excellent Technical Intent should be complete enough for Agents like Cursor and Claude Code to autonomously complete development tasks based on it, while humans only need to review and accept.

      In the next chapter, we will further compare the core differences between old and new software engineering paradigms, deeply understanding the far-reaching impact and significance of this transformation.
